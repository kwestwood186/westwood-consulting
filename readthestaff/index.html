The method, in five moves

Each one is something the learning-science literature is unusually clear about. This course is built around all five.

1 · Anchor, then read by interval

Memorise a handful of landmark notes, then read every other note as a step or skip from the nearest anchor. Fluent readers process notation in chunks — they don't spell each note.

2 · Quiz, don't study

Retrieval practice (testing yourself) and spaced practice are the two highest-utility learning techniques known. So this course makes you produce answers, then spaces them out — it never just shows you a chart to stare at.

3 · Interleave the clefs

Mixing treble and bass in one session feels harder and lowers your in-session score — but it roughly triples what you remember a week later. A textbook "desirable difficulty."

4 · See – hear – play

Every card here plays the note, and you can play it on the on-screen piano or your own. Binding the symbol to a sound and a key is the biggest advantage you have over a paper-only learner.

5 · Accuracy first, then speed

Like a typing tutor: get it right reliably, then push for speed. The timed Speed mode in the Drill stays locked until your accuracy earns it.

A daily routine that works

  • 5–10 min, most days > one long weekly cram.
  • Run The Drill with treble + bass mixed.
  • Expect to feel slower than blocked practice — that discomfort is the learning.
  • Self-test once a week on the Progress page.
One honest warning up front. Mnemonics (FACE, "Every Good Boy…") and writing letter names under notes feel productive but keep you decoding serially — they cap your ceiling. Use a mnemonic for about a week if you like, then let landmarks and intervals replace it. And skip "brain-training" apps entirely: you get better at reading notes by reading notes.
Phase 0 · Orient

Landmarks: a few anchors instead of 30 facts

You don't need to memorise every line and space. You need a small set of landmark notes you know instantly — then you reach any other note by counting steps and skips from the nearest one. Fewer things to learn, and it scales cleanly onto the ledger lines above and below the staff.

The treble and bass staves are really one big staff — the grand staff — joined in the middle by Middle C. That single shared note is your master anchor.

The grand staff. Highlighted: the three anchors worth knowing cold.

Meet the anchors

Tap one to see it on the staff and hear it. Notice why each clef is named after a note.

How to actually use them

  • Middle C sits on its own little ledger line between the two staves.
  • Treble = G clef. Its curl wraps the G line (2nd line up).
  • Bass = F clef. Its two dots hug the F line (4th line up).
  • To read any note: find the nearest anchor, then count steps (next line/space) and skips.
Mnemonics (open only if you want a one-week crutch)

Treble lines E G B D F = "Every Good Boy Does Fine"; treble spaces spell F A C E. Bass lines G B D F A = "Good Boys Do Fine Always"; bass spaces A C E G = "All Cows Eat Grass".

But: reciting a chain to reach one note is slow and disconnects the symbol from its sound. Treat this as a scaffold for ~a week, then drop it. Landmarks + intervals are the real method.

Phase 1 · The actual target skill

Read the distance, not the letter

Fluent reading is interval reading: you see that a note is a step (2nd) or a skip (3rd) up or down from where your eye already is, and you read the shape rather than spelling each note. On the staff the geometry is friendly:

  • 2nd (step): line → the very next space (or vice-versa). Neighbours.
  • 3rd (skip): line → next line, or space → next space. "Stacked."
  • 4th / 5th: bigger reaches you'll start to recognise as single shapes.

Aim to subitise small distances — see "a third" the way you see three dots as "3" without counting.

Phase 3 · Build the chunk library

From single notes to shapes you recognise at a glance

This is the same mechanism that makes chess masters fast: experts store patterns, not individual pieces. Your job now is to stop reading triads and scale fragments note-by-note and start seeing them as single units — a "stack of thirds," a "three-step run."

Why this course is built the way it is

The design isn't a matter of taste — each choice maps to a well-supported finding. Here's the short version, with the evidence behind it.

It's chunking, not decoding

Fluent readers "do not read the score note-by-note… but infer the identity of the notes by musical knowledge," processing sequences holistically. The same chunking mechanism explains chess expertise. So we train pattern recognition, not serial spelling. [1][2]

Reading runs ahead in chunks

Strong readers keep ~7 notes "in flight" ahead of the hands vs ~4 for weak readers, and that span flexes to musical phrasing. Hence: look ahead and group, don't fixate on one notehead. [3]

Notation reading is trainable — and specific

Experts take in a wider region of the page and show reduced visual crowding for notation only. Good news (your adult brain can build it) and a guardrail (generic visual / "brain" training won't transfer). [4][10]

Test & space — the two best techniques

Practice-testing and distributed practice are rated highest utility across ages and subjects. Spaced retrieval beat massed by ~45 points in adults. So: quiz, don't study; short and daily, not one long cram. [5][6]

Interleaving ≈ triples retention

Mixing problem types tripled delayed-test scores (63% vs 20%) despite worse practice performance. That's why both clefs are mixed by default — and why it feels harder. [7][8]

Dual coding at the keyboard

Pairing the visual symbol with sound and a finger position is your biggest lever as a learner with a piano. Every card plays the note; the keyboard drill binds it to the hand. [4]

What to avoid

  • Over-relying on mnemonics / letter-name annotation — both keep you decoding serially. ~1 week, then drop.
  • Blocked, comfort-zone practice (all treble, then all bass) — good practice scores, poor retention.
  • Speed before accuracy — gate on accuracy first, then push speed.
  • "Brain-training" / dual-n-back apps — a 87-study meta-analysis found no reliable far-transfer. [10]
  • Marathon cram sessions — spacing beats massing decisively.
