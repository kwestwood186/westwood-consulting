# Interview Q&A — Draft for Approval

**Instructions:** Read each. Flag any you want reworded, removed, or strengthened. I'll revise then add to the site. The structure for each: *Why they're asking → Framework → Sample answer → Don't do.*

A recurring framework I lean on is **STAR**: Situation, Task, Action, Result. Use it for any "tell me about a time" question. Keep the Situation/Task short; spend most words on Action and Result.

---

## Opening Questions

### 1. "Tell me about yourself."

**Why they're asking:** They want a 60–90 second story that frames why you're the right person for *this* role. They are not asking for your life story.

**Framework:** Present → Past → Future. Where you are now (1 sentence), the 1–2 experiences that got you here (2–3 sentences), what you're looking for next and why this role fits (1–2 sentences).

**Sample answer:** "I'm currently an operations manager at [Company], where I lead a team of eight and own our weekly throughput metrics. Before that, I spent four years in logistics, which is where I learned how to run lean processes under pressure. I'm looking for a role where I can take what I've learned about systems and apply it at a larger scale — which is what drew me to this position at [Company]."

**Don't:** Start with where you were born. Don't list every job. Don't ramble past 90 seconds.

---

### 2. "Walk me through your resume."

**Why they're asking:** Similar to #1 but they want the connective tissue between roles — why you moved each time, what you learned.

**Framework:** Chronological, but lead with *why* you took each role and *what you accomplished*, not the job description. Each role: 2 sentences max.

**Sample answer:** "I started in customer service at [Company A] because I wanted to learn how a business actually works from the customer's side. After two years, I moved into operations because I saw how much waste there was in how we handled tickets — and I wanted to fix it. That led me to my current role, where I now own the entire workflow."

**Don't:** Read your resume out loud. They have it.

---

### 3. "Why are you interested in this role?"

**Why they're asking:** They want to know if you applied to 200 jobs or you actually thought about *theirs*.

**Framework:** Connect a specific thing about the company (mission, product, recent news) to a specific thing about your goals or background. This is where your cover letter research pays off.

**Sample answer:** "Two reasons. First, I've been a customer of [Company] for three years and I've watched you grow — what stood out to me was [specific thing]. Second, the part of this role focused on [specific responsibility] is exactly the work I want to spend the next phase of my career on, because [reason]."

**Don't:** Say "it's a great company" or "I want to grow." Generic answers signal you didn't do the work.

## Behavioral Questions (STAR)

### 4. "Tell me about a time you failed."

**Why they're asking:** They want to see self-awareness and learning, not perfection. Candidates who can't name a failure look either dishonest or unreflective.

**Framework:** Pick a real failure that was *yours* (not your team's). Spend 70% of the answer on what you learned and changed afterward.

**Sample answer:** "Early in my role at [Company], I launched a process change without consulting the floor staff first. It improved metrics on paper but created a bottleneck I hadn't seen because I didn't talk to the people doing the work. Within two weeks, throughput dropped. I rolled it back, ran small group conversations with every shift, and re-launched a version that worked. Since then, I never roll out a process change without first walking the floor with the people it affects."

**Don't:** Use a fake-failure like "I work too hard." Don't blame others. Don't pick a failure so catastrophic it disqualifies you.

---

### 5. "Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a coworker."

**Why they're asking:** Every workplace has conflict. They want to see you handle it like an adult — not avoid it, not escalate it.

**Framework:** Focus on the resolution, not the drama. Describe what the disagreement was *about* (not who was right), how you approached the person, what you both agreed to.

**Sample answer:** "A peer and I disagreed about how to prioritize incoming requests — they wanted first-come-first-served, I wanted a triage system based on urgency. Instead of going to our manager, I asked them to grab coffee, and I came in genuinely curious about why they preferred their approach. It turned out they'd been burned by a previous system that felt arbitrary. We ended up co-designing a triage rubric so the rules were transparent. We've used it for two years."

**Don't:** Pick a conflict where the other person was the villain. Don't make it personal.

---

### 6. "Describe a time you led a project."

**Why they're asking:** They want to see initiative, planning, and the ability to get others to follow you — formally or informally.

**Framework:** STAR. Be specific about what you *did*, not what the team did. Use "I" more than "we."

**Sample answer:** "We needed to migrate our scheduling system off an old tool that was costing us $40K a year. I volunteered to lead it because no one else wanted to. I scoped the project into three phases, brought in stakeholders from each affected team, and ran a weekly 15-minute sync to keep us on track. We finished in 11 weeks, two weeks ahead of plan, and the new tool cut our scheduling errors by about 30%."

**Don't:** Pick a project so small it doesn't matter. Don't take credit for work that wasn't yours.

---

### 7. "Tell me about a time you had to learn something quickly."

**Why they're asking:** They're testing how you handle being out of your depth — which you *will* be in any new job.

**Framework:** Show your learning method, not just the result. Did you find a mentor? Take a course? Reverse-engineer something?

**Sample answer:** "When I took over operations, I had no background in inventory management — but I owned the warehouse. I gave myself two weeks. I spent the first week shadowing every role on the floor for half a day each. The second week, I read two books recommended by an industry peer, and I shadowed our best-performing team's stand-ups. By week three, I could speak the language well enough to start asking the right questions. Six months in, I'd cut our missed-shipment rate by half."

**Don't:** Pretend you learned everything overnight. The honesty about the process is what makes it credible.

---

### 8. "Describe a time you went above and beyond."

**Why they're asking:** They want to see what your default work ethic looks like — and whether you measure your work by hours or by impact.

**Framework:** Pick a moment where you *chose* to do more than your job description, and the result mattered. Don't pick "I worked overtime" — that's just labor, not initiative.

**Sample answer:** "We had a customer escalation on a Friday afternoon that wasn't technically in my queue. I could have left it for Monday, but the customer was a long-term account at risk of leaving. I stayed late, called them directly, found out the real issue was a billing error on our side, and got finance to issue a credit before end of day. We kept the account. I wasn't asked to do this — I just knew it mattered."

**Don't:** Use "I work weekends" as your example. That's a red flag for burnout, not a strength.

---

### 9. "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager."

**Why they're asking:** They want to know if you push back when needed (good) or roll over (bad), but also that you can disagree *professionally*.

**Framework:** Disagreement → respectful conversation → outcome. The outcome can be either: you persuaded them, or they made the call and you supported it fully. Both are good answers.

**Sample answer:** "My manager wanted to roll out a new shift schedule across all teams in one week. I thought we needed a pilot first because the last time we rushed a change, we lost three people. I asked for 20 minutes, laid out my concerns and the data, and proposed a two-week pilot with one team. He agreed to the pilot. It surfaced two issues we'd never have caught at scale, and the full rollout went smoothly."

**Don't:** Frame it as "my manager was wrong." Frame it as "we had different perspectives and worked through it."

---

### 10. "Describe a time you missed a deadline."

**Why they're asking:** Similar to "tell me about a failure." They want accountability and learning.

**Framework:** Own it. Explain what happened, what you did *the moment you realized you'd miss it* (the communication is what matters), and what changed afterward.

**Sample answer:** "I committed to a process audit deliverable in four weeks. By week two, I realized I'd underestimated the data-gathering phase by about a week. As soon as I knew, I told my manager — not at the deadline, but with two weeks to spare — and we agreed on a revised date. I delivered on the revised date and started building in a 20% time buffer on every project since."

**Don't:** Blame external factors. Don't pretend you've never missed one.

---

### 11. "Tell me about a time you received difficult feedback."

**Why they're asking:** They want to see emotional maturity and growth, not defensiveness.

**Framework:** Specific feedback → your initial reaction (honesty matters here) → what you did with it → what changed.

**Sample answer:** "In a 360 review, several reports said I gave them too little context when I delegated. My first reaction was defensiveness — I thought I was empowering them by not micromanaging. After I sat with it, I realized 'no context' isn't empowerment, it's abandonment. I started writing a short 'what good looks like' note for every delegated task. The next review, that score went up significantly."

**Don't:** Pick feedback that's actually a humble-brag ("I work too hard"). Pick real feedback.

## Strengths, Weaknesses, and Fit

### 12. "What's your greatest weakness?"

**Why they're asking:** They want self-awareness and active growth — not a fake answer dressed up as humility.

**Framework:** Pick a real, non-dealbreaking weakness. Show what you're doing about it. Don't pick a strength in disguise.

**Sample answer:** "I tend to push too hard on details when a project is 95% done. I've had to learn that 'shipped and good' beats 'unshipped and perfect.' I've gotten better by setting a hard time-box on final polish and asking a peer to gut-check whether more refinement actually improves the outcome."

**Don't:** Say "I'm a perfectionist," "I work too hard," or "I care too much." Hiring managers have heard these 10,000 times.

---

### 13. "What's your greatest strength?"

**Why they're asking:** They want one strength backed by evidence — not three vague adjectives.

**Framework:** Pick a strength that maps directly to the job. Back it up with a one-sentence example.

**Sample answer:** "My strongest skill is building processes that scale. At [Company], I took a manual workflow that broke every time we grew — and rebuilt it into a system that handled 3x the volume with the same headcount. The reason I'm good at this is I treat process design like product design: who's the user, what's their job to be done, what's the simplest thing that works."

**Don't:** Say "I'm a team player" or "I'm a hard worker." Both true of most people. Neither memorable.

---

### 14. "Why should we hire you?"

**Why they're asking:** They want you to close the sale. This is a gift of a question — they're handing you the floor.

**Framework:** 3 specific reasons. Map each to something in the job description or something they care about.

**Sample answer:** "Three reasons. First, the part of this role that requires [specific skill] — I've done that exact work for five years and I can show you results. Second, I've worked in companies at your stage before and I know what scales and what breaks. Third, I'm at a point in my career where I'm not job-hopping — I want to settle into a role and build something. The combination of those three is what makes me right for this seat specifically."

**Don't:** Say "I'm passionate" or "I'm a quick learner." Both undifferentiated.

---

### 15. "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

**Why they're asking:** They want to know if your trajectory matches what they can offer. They're not asking you to predict the future.

**Framework:** Direction over destination. Talk about the *kind* of work you want to be doing and the *kind* of impact you want to be having. Tie it to a path that's plausible at this company.

**Sample answer:** "In five years, I want to be leading a larger operations team — probably 20–30 people — at a company where my work directly affects the customer experience. I'm not someone who maps out 'I want to be VP by 33.' I'd rather get deep at one or two companies and build real expertise, which is part of why this role appeals to me — there's enough scope here to grow into for a long time."

**Don't:** Say "in your job" (creepy) or "I haven't thought about it" (lazy).

## Tough / Curveball Questions

### 16. "Why are you leaving your current job?"

**Why they're asking:** They're watching for red flags — do you badmouth your employer, are you running *from* something or *toward* something.

**Framework:** Always frame as "running toward." Be honest about what's missing without trashing your current employer.

**Sample answer:** "I've gotten what I came for in my current role — I built the team, fixed the process gaps, and the work is now running smoothly. The next set of problems I want to solve are at a different scale, and my current company isn't growing into them in the timeline I'd want. So I'm looking outward for that next challenge."

**Don't:** Say "my boss is terrible," "the culture is toxic," or anything that makes you sound like the problem. Even if it's true.

---

### 17. "What's your salary expectation?"

**Why they're asking:** They want to know if your number is in their range. They're also testing how prepared you are.

**Framework:** Give a specific number based on research, not a range. (See Salary Negotiation page for the full method.) If pushed earlier than you'd like to share, deflect once: "I'd like to understand the role better before I name a number — can we come back to that?" If they push again, give your number.

**Sample answer:** "Based on what I've researched for roles at this level in this region — and my eight years of experience — I'm looking for $XX,XXX. I'm open to discussing the full package, but that's my anchor."

**Don't:** Give a range ($90–110K means they hear $90K). Don't say "I'm flexible." Don't ask "what's the budget?" first — it makes you look unprepared.

---

### 18. "Why is there a gap in your resume?" / "Why were you laid off?"

**Why they're asking:** They want a non-defensive, honest answer. Gaps and layoffs are normal — what they're really testing is your composure.

**Framework:** State the reason in one sentence. State what you did during the time in one sentence. Pivot back to why you're ready now.

**Sample answer (gap):** "I took a year off to care for a family member. During that time, I kept my skills sharp by [taking a course / consulting on a project / volunteering with X]. I'm fully ready to come back and excited to do it in a role like this one."

**Sample answer (layoff):** "My company restructured and eliminated my whole team. It wasn't related to performance — I have references from my manager who can confirm that. Since then, I've been [specific thing], and I'm now looking for the right next role."

**Don't:** Apologize. Don't over-explain. Don't get emotional.

## Questions YOU Should Ask the Interviewer

You should walk in with 5–7 prepared questions. Always ask at least 3. Asking nothing makes you look uninterested.

### 19. "Do you have any hesitations about my candidacy for this position?"

**Why this is powerful:** It's Kevin's favorite — and for good reason. It does three things at once:
1. It surfaces objections so you can address them *in the room*, not after the interview when it's too late.
2. It signals confidence — most candidates are too scared to ask.
3. It gives the interviewer permission to be honest, which builds trust.

**When to ask:** Near the end of the interview, after rapport is built.

**How to handle the response:** If they name a concern, don't get defensive. Say "That's fair — let me address that" and provide a concrete example or piece of evidence that counters it. If they say "no hesitations," you've ended on a strong note.

---

### 20. "What does success look like in this role at 30, 60, and 90 days?"

**Why this is powerful:** It shifts you from candidate to "future employee." It also surfaces whether the hiring manager has actually thought about onboarding — which tells you a lot about the company.

**How to handle the response:** Take notes. If you get the job, this is your roadmap for the first quarter. If their answer is vague ("just get up to speed"), that's a flag — they may not have a clear plan for you.

---

**Bonus questions to keep in your back pocket:**
- "What's the biggest challenge facing the team right now?"
- "How would you describe your management style?"
- "What separates someone who's good in this role from someone who's great?"
- "What do you wish someone had told you before you joined?"
